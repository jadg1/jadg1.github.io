Derisking European Critical Raw Material Dependencies with Graph Learning.

  • Analyzes extra-EU imports of 16 strategic critical raw materials (1995–2023)
  • Gravity models reveal the EU imports from distant, linguistically different, diplomatically misaligned countries — the opposite of what trade theory predicts
  • Discrete-Time Dynamic Graph architecture models country-level disruption scenarios

Gravity model — who does the EU actually import from?
EU-destination models on 16 strategic CRMs (1995–2023)
Standard trade gravity predicts countries trade most with partners that are close, culturally similar, and diplomatically aligned. For EU critical raw material imports, the opposite holds — the EU sources these materials from exactly the kind of countries we would expect it not to.
Distance
Very negative & significant
Expected: small negative. Found: strongly negative for EU imports — suppliers like China, South Africa, and Brazil are far away
Contiguity
Negative, insignificant
Expected: positive. Sharing a border doesn’t help — though EU-level contiguity (Russia via Baltics, Finland) may matter more than country-level
Common language
Negative
Expected: positive. Top CRM suppliers (Turkey, China, Thailand) don’t share EU official languages
Diplomatic disagreement
Positive
Expected: negative. The EU imports CRMs from countries that vote against it in international bodies — Russia, China, Turkey
The EU’s most strategic material dependencies sit with distant, linguistically different, and diplomatically misaligned suppliers. This structural mismatch between geopolitical alignment and resource dependence is what the paper investigates.

MicroClinic: An ultra-low-parameter neural network for medical image analysis
