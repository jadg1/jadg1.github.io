01 / Banking Finance Thesis

Does Cross-Border Common Ownership Diversify Bank Risk?

  • Tests whether shared institutional shareholders transmit systemic risk between European banks — a contagion channel the macroprudential framework barely touches
  • Distinguishes cross-border from domestic common ownership in the EU banking sector — a separation absent from prior work
  • Speaks directly to the live Banking Union debate on integration vs. contagion
Supervised by Albert Banal-Estañol
In progress · 2026
The contagion channel nobody is monitoring
EU banks · cross-border vs domestic common ownership
Banking Union supervision tracks lending and interbank exposures. But two banks in different countries can also be linked by a third party — a large institutional investor that owns meaningful stakes in both. If that investor reallocates, both banks move together.
What's new
An empirical separation prior work does not make
Existing studies treat institutional ownership as one number. We split it into the part that crosses borders and the part that stays at home — and ask whether the geography of ownership matters for systemic risk.
— Decomposition
Cross-border vs domestic ownership
Splits common ownership by investor geography rather than treating it as a single aggregate.
— Scope
European banking sector
Brings the common-ownership literature into the Banking Union debate, where the geographic dimension is central.
— Robustness
Supervisory perimeter test
Tests whether the EBA boundary marks a meaningful economic frontier for the contagion channel.
— Frame
Ownership-aware integration
Re-frames financial integration around shareholders, not just lending and interbank exposures.
Why it matters for policy
Implications for macroprudential supervision in the EU
The Buch position — deepen integration but manage contagion — is broadly supported. But contagion need not arrive only through the channels currently monitored. Shared shareholders are a parallel route the macroprudential framework barely covers.
  1. Ownership transparency belongs in the macroprudential toolkit
    Supervisors already track lending and capital. The investor side of the balance sheet deserves the same visibility.
  2. Stress tests should include common-investor scenarios
    Standard scenarios capture asset shocks. They should also capture coordinated reallocation by shared shareholders.
  3. Banking Union integration should be ownership-aware
    The supervisory perimeter alone may not capture where contagion travels. Integration policy needs to look through to the investor base.
The takeaway. Integration is desirable; contagion is the side-effect. Watching only the lending side leaves a blind spot on the equity side.
02 / Trade Quantitative Economics Thesis · Working Paper

Derisking European Critical Raw Material Dependencies with Graph Learning

  • Combines structural trade economics with graph machine learning to study the EU's exposure to critical raw materials
  • Asks where the EU sources strategic materials, and what happens if a major supplier becomes unavailable
  • Produces a risk taxonomy aligned with the EU 2030 Critical Raw Materials Act
Supervised by Santiago Pérez Balsalobre and Javier Bas
Working paper · 2026
A structural mismatch in EU sourcing
Where strategic dependencies actually sit
Trade theory predicts that countries source most from partners that are nearby, culturally close, and politically aligned. For the materials the EU's green and digital transitions depend on, the pattern is roughly the opposite.
What's new
Two literatures, one question
Trade economics tells us why patterns hold. Graph learning tells us what happens when they break. The thesis combines the two so policy questions about EU material security can be answered with both.
— Methods bridge
Structural trade + graph learning
Brings dynamic graph neural networks into a domain dominated by gravity models, without abandoning the structural intuition.
— Object
Trade as an evolving network
Treats the global CRM trade system as a dynamic graph rather than a stack of bilateral flows, making relational structure first-class.
— Counterfactuals
Supplier-disruption scenarios
Asks what concentration looks like if a major supplier becomes unavailable — the question policy actually has to answer.
— Output
Policy-aligned risk taxonomy
Translates model outputs into a classification compatible with the EU's own concentration thresholds.
Why it matters for policy
A quantitative complement to the EU 2030 Critical Raw Materials agenda
The EU has set diversification, processing, and recycling targets for the materials its green and digital transitions depend on. The thesis offers a way to prioritize those targets — telling policymakers where the structural exposure is largest before the next disruption forces the answer.
  1. Identify which materials carry the most structural risk
    Move the conversation from "all 16 are critical" to a defensible ranking of where attention is most needed first.
  2. Stress-test dependencies before they break
    Quantify what supplier-by-supplier disruption would do to market concentration, in advance of the political moment that makes it urgent.
  3. Frame de-risking as a structural problem
    EU exposure is not random — it sits with distant, politically misaligned partners. De-risking has to confront the geography, not just the volumes.
The takeaway. Resource security is not a list of materials. It's a network with structural weak points — and those weak points can be located before they fail.
