MSc Finance Thesis · UPF/BSM

Does Cross-Border Common Ownership Diversify Bank Risk?

  • Decomposes common ownership into cross-border (XB) and domestic (DOM) components for the EU25 — top European banks by assets, 654 bank-years (2003–2023)
  • Tests whether cross-border common ownership transmits systemic risk — a channel the macroprudential framework barely touches
  • Two ownership measures: C (investor overlap) and λ (O'Brien–Salop incentive weight), both decomposable XB / DOM
  • Cross-border common ownership predicts higher systemic risk; domestic does not
  • EBA-perimeter robustness: integration under EU supervision is not safer than integration with non-EU investors

MSc QE Thesis · UAM · Working Paper

Derisking European Critical Raw Material Dependencies with Graph Learning

  • Analyzes extra-EU imports of 16 strategic critical raw materials (1995–2023)
  • Gravity models reveal the EU imports from distant, linguistically different, diplomatically misaligned countries — the opposite of what trade theory predicts
  • Discrete-Time Dynamic Graph architecture models country-level disruption scenarios
  • Combines HHI concentration, dominant supplier dependency, and structural risk indicators
  • Policy-relevant risk classification aligned with EU 2030 CRM goals
Supervised by Santiago Pérez Balsalobre and Javier Bas

Gravity model — who does the EU actually import from?
EU-destination models on 16 strategic CRMs (1995–2023)
Standard trade gravity predicts countries trade most with partners that are close, culturally similar, and diplomatically aligned. For EU critical raw material imports, the opposite holds.
Distance
Very negative & sig.
Suppliers like China, South Africa, Brazil are far away — strongly negative for CRM imports.
Contiguity
Negative, insig.
Sharing a border doesn't help — though EU-level contiguity may matter more than country-level.
Common language
Negative
Top CRM suppliers (Turkey, China, Thailand) don't share EU official languages.
Diplomatic disagreement
Positive
EU imports CRMs from countries that vote against it — Russia, China, Turkey.
The EU's most strategic material dependencies sit with distant, linguistically different, and diplomatically misaligned suppliers. This structural mismatch between geopolitical alignment and resource dependence is what the paper investigates.
